Plain language rule: A source fact does not automatically prove a verdict. If you use a word like great, rewarding, best, or worth it, you need to define it, measure it, or limit it.

Step 2

Verdict words to watch

These words are not banned. They just need proof, definition, or a more precise replacement.

Judgment words

great rewarding worth it better / best meaningful interesting important cool smart

Overclaim words

always never anyone everyone proves shows, when you mean suggests

Step 3

Fix the fact to verdict leap

Use these stems when you feel yourself trying to jump from a fact to a vibe. The goal is not to make your writing less confident. The goal is to make the confidence easier to prove.

fact → verdict
Main move A source fact can support your idea, but it cannot do all the thinking for you. Add the reason, the limit, or the tradeoff so the reader can see how your evidence connects to your claim.
Instead of “great”Try: “offers strong demand, though it requires...”
Instead of “rewarding”Try: “some workers report satisfaction because..., though...”
Instead of “best way”Try: “one effective way is...”
Instead of “proves”Try: “suggests,” “indicates,” or “points to.”
Instead of “always” or “anyone”Try: “often,” “many,” “some,” “in some settings,” or “for students who...”
Quick upgrade move: When you want to say “great,” force yourself to name two tradeoffs. Try this sentence shape: “This career offers ___, but it also requires ___.”

Step 5

Rewrite one sentence from your draft

Pick one sentence from your draft essay that includes a verdict word. Rewrite it so the claim becomes specific, careful, and easier to support with evidence.

Use evidence language like “suggests” or “indicates” instead of “proves.”

Make “great” measurable

Find one sentence in your own draft where you use the word “great.” If you do not use that exact word, choose a sentence where you make a similar judgment, such as “good,” “best,” “smart,” “rewarding,” or “worth it.”
