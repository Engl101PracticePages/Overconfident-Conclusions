1) Sample paragraph (verdict / overconfident conclusions)

Click a highlighted word/phrase to get rewrite options.

Tip: “Job growth” is a fact. “Great career” is a verdict. A fact doesn’t automatically prove a verdict.

What’s wrong here (plain terms):
  • It uses verdict words like great, stable, smart, rewarding, worth it, better.
  • It treats facts as if they automatically prove those verdicts.
  • It overclaims with phrases like always (usually too broad).
The rule: If you use a verdict word, you must do at least one:
  • Define it (what do you mean by “great”? pay? schedule? job satisfaction?)
  • Measure it (use evidence you can point to)
  • Limit it (great for who, under what conditions?)

2) Verdict words to watch

These aren’t “banned.” They just need proof or a more precise replacement.

Verdict words

  • great
  • rewarding
  • worth it
  • better / best
  • meaningful
  • interesting
  • important
  • cool
  • smart (as a judgment)

Overclaim words

  • always
  • never
  • anyone
  • everyone
  • proves
  • shows (when it’s really “suggests”)

3) Fix the “fact → verdict” leap

Use these stems when you feel yourself trying to jump from a fact to a vibe.

  • Instead of “great,” try: “offers strong demand, though it requires…”
  • Instead of “rewarding,” try: “some workers report satisfaction because…, though…”
  • Instead of “best way,” try: “one effective way is…”
  • Instead of “proves,” try: “suggests,” “indicates,” “points to”
  • Instead of “always/anyone,” try: “often,” “many,” “some,” “in some settings,” “for students who…”

Quick upgrade move

When you want to say “great,” force yourself to name two tradeoffs:
“This career offers ___, but it also requires ___.”

4) Practice set: what kind of sentence is this?

Label each sentence: A defensible claim (specific, measurable, or careful) • B fact → verdict leap (needs proof/definition) • C overclaim (too broad: always/anyone/etc.)

Answer key (with quick why):
  1. B — job growth doesn’t automatically prove “great.”
  2. A — specific consequence (training cost/time) without verdict.
  3. B — “rewarding” is a verdict without evidence.
  4. A — describes conditions; no leap.
  5. C — “anyone” overclaim + “cool” is vague.
  6. A — limited to a type of person + specific requirements.
  7. B — “stable and smart” are judgments; needs evidence + definition.
  8. C — “always” + “never” language is too absolute.
